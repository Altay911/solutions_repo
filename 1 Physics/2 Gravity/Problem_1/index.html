Kepler's Third Law: Orbital Period and Radius

Theoretical Foundations

Derivation of Kepler's Third Law

For a circular orbit with: - Orbital radius \(r\) - Orbital period \(T\) - Central mass \(M\) - Orbiting mass \(m\) (where \(m \ll M\))

Centripetal force = Gravitational force:

\[ \frac{mv^2}{r} = \frac{GMm}{r^2} \]

Orbital velocity:

\[ v = \frac{2\pi r}{T} \]

Substituting gives Kepler's Third Law:

\[ T^2 = \left( \frac{4\pi^2}{GM} \right) r^3 \]

Key Implications

  1. Mass Determination: $$ M = \frac{4\pi^2 r^3}{GT^2} $$

  2. Scale Invariance: The ratio \(T^2/r^3\) is constant for all bodies orbiting the same mass

  3. Generalization to Ellipses: Replace \(r\) with semi-major axis \(a\) for elliptical orbits

Solar System Examples

Body Orbital Radius (AU) Period (years) \(T^2/a^3\) (yr²/AU³)
Mercury 0.387 0.241 1.000
Venus 0.723 0.615 0.999
Earth 1.000 1.000 1.000
Mars 1.524 1.881 1.000
Jupiter 5.203 11.86 0.999

Data confirms the constant ratio predicted by Kepler

Computational Verification

Circular Orbit Simulation Approach

  1. Numerical Integration: Solve the equations of motion: $$ \frac{d^2\mathbf{r}}{dt^2} = -\frac{GM}{r^3}\mathbf{r} $$

  2. Verification Steps:

  3. Simulate orbits with varying \(r\)
  4. Measure resulting periods \(T\)
  5. Plot \(T^2\) vs \(r^3\) to verify linearity

Expected Results

  1. Orbit Visualization:
  2. Perfectly circular trajectories

  3. Constant orbital velocity

  4. Kepler Verification Plot:

  5. Straight line on \(T^2\) vs \(r^3\) graph
  6. Slope = \(4\pi^2/GM\)

Extensions to Real Systems

Elliptical Orbits

  1. Modified Law: $$ T^2 = \left( \frac{4\pi^2}{GM} \right) a^3 $$ Where \(a\) is semi-major axis

  2. Eccentricity Effects:

  3. Period depends only on \(a\), not eccentricity
  4. Velocity varies throughout orbit

Binary Systems

For comparable masses \(M_1\) and \(M_2\): $$ T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2 a^3}{G(M_1+M_2)} $$

Practical Applications

  1. Exoplanet Detection:

  2. Measure star's wobble period to determine planet's orbital distance

  3. Satellite Deployment:

  4. Calculate geostationary orbit radius (~42,164 km)

  5. Galactic Dynamics:

  6. Estimate mass distribution in galaxies

Limitations

  1. Perturbation Effects:

  2. Multi-body systems deviate from ideal law

  3. Relativistic Corrections:

  4. Needed for orbits close to massive objects

  5. Non-Point Masses:

  6. Tidal forces and oblateness affect orbits
